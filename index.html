Abstract

Small everyday objects such as keys, earbuds, chargers, and phones are frequently misplaced in realistic indoor environments. Recovering them from video is difficult because they are visually inconspicuous, weakly textured, easily occluded, and often surrounded by clutter. Existing benchmarks partially evaluate related capabilities such as video memory, object grounding, tracking, and spatial reasoning, but they do not directly assess whether a model can recover the last known location of a specified small object and describe it in an actionable way for real-world retrieval.

In this work, we formalize this problem as target-driven small-object recovery in video. We decompose it into a progressive capability chain including target presence verification, reference-based localization, fine-grained recovery description, and structured actionable grounding. Based on this formulation, we introduce a new benchmark centered on small household objects in indoor videos and study whether current multimodal models can recover such targets under clutter, partial occlusion, weak saliency, and long temporal gaps. The benchmark is designed to distinguish between recognizing that an object appears, roughly locating it, describing it precisely, and producing an output that is directly usable for downstream search.

Motivation

In everyday environments, users often need more than generic video understanding. What matters is whether a system can reason about a known target object: whether it appeared, where it ended up, which nearby objects provide useful cues, and whether this information can be expressed clearly for downstream use.

Compared with larger objects such as backpacks or televisions, small items like keys and earbuds are much more likely to "disappear" in indoor environments. They are physically small, visually less salient, easily occluded, and often surrounded by clutter. Even when they appeared only a short time earlier, people may still struggle to recall their exact final locations. This difficulty is consistent with the basic mechanisms of human visual cognition—the human brain does not process all visual information in a scene equally, but instead gives priority to the parts that are most relevant to the current goal.

This problem is becoming increasingly relevant in practice. With the development of wearable assistants, egocentric devices, and multimodal agents, recovering target objects from video is gradually becoming a meaningful real-world task. Simply recognizing the object category or identifying a rough scene region is not sufficient. The system must also produce spatial outputs that can directly support real-world search. However, existing benchmarks—including Ego4D, VSI-Bench, EgoTracks, and ReferDINO—do not directly answer a practical question: given a known small object, can a model recover its last known location from a long video and express it in a sufficiently precise and actionable manner?

PinpointQA Task Formulation

To evaluate target-centered video spatial understanding for daily small objects, we define four tasks that together form a progressive capability chain—moving from target perception to reference grounding, natural-language localization, and structured spatial representation.

Target Presence Verification (TPV)

Given a video and a target object, TPV asks the model to determine whether the target appears in the video. This task focuses on the most basic level of target perception, especially in indoor scenes where small objects may be hard to notice due to clutter, occlusion, or viewpoint changes. TPV serves as the starting point of the task chain, since all later stages rely on first detecting the target correctly.

Nearest Reference Identification (NRI)

After confirming the presence of the target, NRI asks the model to identify the reference object that is closest to the target. The goal is not simple object recognition, but whether the model can correctly judge local spatial proximity between the target and surrounding objects. NRI connects target perception with later spatial expression, marking the key step from seeing the target to understanding its local spatial context.

Fine-Grained Spatial Description (FSD)

FSD requires the model to describe the location of the target object in natural language based on the video. The description should be as precise as possible and should use spatial cues such as the supporting surface, nearby reference objects, relative directions, and distances. Unlike earlier tasks, FSD requires the model to combine multiple local cues and produce a clear and accurate localization statement for human users.

Structured Spatial Prediction (SSP)

Building on FSD, SSP further requires the model to organize the target location into a structured output rather than free-form text. The model is expected to provide key spatial fields directly useful for localization, including the supporting object, nearby reference objects, and their corresponding distances. As the final stage, SSP places the strongest demand on the model's ability to organize spatial information.

Data Construction Pipeline

Figure 2. Benchmark construction pipeline. Starting from large-scale indoor 3D scene datasets, we progressively build grounded QA pairs for our four-task formulation.

Data Sources. Our scenes are drawn from ScanNet++ and ScanNet200, which satisfy two main requirements: they contain rich instance-level annotations for small objects, and they reflect an indoor roaming observation pattern that is close to everyday object search, where an observer moves through a room and gradually finds and localizes a target from different viewpoints.

Scene Curation. We use a pre-selected small-object vocabulary to identify candidate target objects, then combine segmentation results with aligned 3D meshes to build object-level geometric representations. The pipeline filters nearby objects by centroid distance (keeping those within 1.0 m by default), then computes fine-grained 3D spatial relations between the target and its nearby candidates, including edge distance, center distance, horizontal distance, vertical offset, and relative above-below relations.

Task-specific QA Generation. Based on the intermediate target-centered representation, we generate QA pairs for all four tasks. For TPV, we construct presence questions with both positive and negative samples. For NRI, we build multiple-choice questions from local reference objects. For FSD, we organize free-form answers guided by supporting objects, nearby references, and centimeter-level distances. For SSP, we further organize the same information into a directly parsable JSON schema.

Quality Control. We adopt a quality control process that combines automatic filtering with manual review. This includes removing invalid labels, enforcing uniqueness constraints, applying task-specific validity constraints, and conducting multiple rounds of manual spot-checking to iteratively refine the QA generation logic.

Benchmark Statistics

Figure 3. Data statistics of the benchmark from four perspectives: (a) task distribution, (b) data-source composition, (c) target object categories, and (d) video duration distribution.

The benchmark maintains a balanced task distribution: TPV, NRI, FSD, and SSP account for 26.47%, 23.10%, 25.08%, and 25.34% respectively. In terms of data sources, 73.2% of the samples come from ScanNet++, while 26.8% come from ScanNet200. The benchmark covers a wide range of daily small objects, with high-frequency targets including mouse, book, bottle, cup, mug, headphones, power strip, charger, telephone, and notebook.

Regarding video duration, the largest proportion falls in the 30–60s range (19.04%), followed by 60–90s (15.82%), 120–150s (15.72%), and 90–120s (13.18%). Videos within 0–150s account for 67.67% of the benchmark, while longer videos are also retained including durations exceeding 270s (5.27%), ensuring the benchmark covers both short-to-medium indoor search scenarios and more challenging long-duration cases.

Experiments

Experimental Setup

We evaluate three groups of multimodal large language models (MLLMs). Proprietary models include GPT-5.4 and Kimi K2.5. Open-source models include LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B, Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct, InternVL3.5-8B-Instruct, Spatial-MLLM-v1.1-Instruct-820K, SenseNova-SI-1.3-InternVL3-8B, and Cambrian-S-7B. We additionally conduct LoRA fine-tuning on two representative open-source backbones, yielding Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct-SFT and InternVL3.5-8B-Instruct-SFT. The benchmark is partitioned at the scene level into training, validation, and evaluation splits (approximately 6:2:2), yielding 6,121, 1,954, and 2,019 samples respectively.

Key Findings

Overall competitiveness. Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct-SFT achieves the best overall performance (0.48/0.49 Avg Micro/Macro), followed by InternVL3.5-8B-Instruct-SFT (0.45/0.45). Among proprietary models, Kimi K2.5 outperforms GPT-5.4 (0.42/0.44 vs. 0.38/0.40). Notably, among non-fine-tuned open-source models, Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct already exceeds GPT-5.4 in Avg-Micro and matches it in Avg-Macro.

Progressive capability breakdown. A clear pattern is the monotonic decline from TPV to SSP, appearing across almost all models. For example, Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct-SFT drops from 0.83/0.84 on TPV to 0.29/0.29 on SSP, and Kimi K2.5 drops from 0.80/0.84 to 0.15/0.15. This confirms that current MLLMs do not fail at one isolated step. Instead, performance degrades steadily as the task moves from target perception to structured spatial output, directly supporting the meaning of the four-stage task design.

Stage-specific failure profiles. Different models break down at different stages. LLaVA-OneVision-1.5-8B reaches 0.76/0.79 on TPV but only 0.07/0.06 on SSP. In contrast, SenseNova-SI-1.3 attains 0.36/0.40 on NRI yet only 0.15/0.16 on FSD, suggesting a different bottleneck between reference grounding and natural-language localization. These differences indicate that the benchmark can reveal where the failure first occurs.

Training utility. Fine-tuning shows that the benchmark is effective as a training resource. Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct improves from 0.39/0.40 to 0.48/0.49 in Avg, with the largest gains on SSP (0.12→0.29). Even so, SSP remains far below the other tasks, showing that executable spatial grounding is still the most difficult stage for current models.

Conclusion

We introduced a benchmark for target-driven small-object recovery in video, focusing on spatiotemporal localization and fine-grained spatial relation description in realistic indoor environments. By decomposing the problem into four progressively harder tasks, the benchmark distinguishes coarse target awareness from precise, reference-grounded, and actionable recovery. This design makes it possible to evaluate not only whether a model can detect a target, but whether it can support actual retrieval.

More broadly, the benchmark shifts evaluation from generic scene understanding toward a more practical question: can a multimodal system help a user recover a misplaced small object from video? We hope this benchmark will support future work on egocentric memory, target-driven spatial grounding, and multimodal assistants that operate in realistic daily environments.
